On Wednesday May 20th at 7 AM 80,000 employees at Meta will have to roll a ten sided die, and if they roll a one, they’ll die. I think I’ve struggled with the idea of death for a long time, and everytime I bring this up a lot of my friends will tell me a phrase along the same line of: 

“You won't be able to stress about being dead when you’re dead, you’ll be too busy being dead”

But I can’t really grasp that, you know? It’s hard to practice not thinking about the end but inevitably death arrives for us all. The more I anticipate these layoffs the more I feel like my fear of unemployment is the same fear of death wearing a different costume. 

There’s this scene in “The Office” when Michael leaves the office for the last time where he looks back at the office working normally as usual and it always crushes me every re-watch. The truth is our companies existed before we were there and they will continue to exist long after we exit. You would think with all the turmoil this impending doom would cause me to stop caring about what I do, and what feels embarrassing to admit is that I still care. It’s 10 pm and I still passively look at my laptop researching some proposal that might not see the light of day, maybe my way of coping is to work like the inevitable isn’t happening. 

Another part of me realizes on Wednesday morning, when I roll this die, there’s a solid chance my job will just be gone. There won’t be a model for me to worry about, documents to research, proposals to write. The model will continue serving people without me, the remaining coworkers picking up the work I left behind, I’ll just be dead. 

More and more of my friends are facing the same issues in this industry, and it’s becoming clear that this stress is leaking into my everyday life. I think this is good practice for the inevitable and how I handle it. I was doing okay before I had this job, I’ll be okay after it. It’s just hard to grasp the mortality of myself and the things I do.
